The Hebrew Beneath the English

Arum / Erom ‚Äî Craft and Nakedness (vv.1, 7)

Arum (H6175) = "cunning, crafty, subtil." Erom (H5903) = "naked, nudity." Both from the same root aram (H6191). Craftiness and nakedness share a root. The serpent is arum; the humans become erom. The wordplay is deliberate: the most cunning creature leads to the state of total exposure. The craft that promised godlike knowledge produces only the knowledge of one's own vulnerability.

Shuph ‚Äî The First Wound-Word (v.15)

Shuph (H7779) = "to gape, snap at; to overwhelm; to break, bruise, cover." Used for BOTH parties: "it (her seed) shall shuph thy head, and thou shalt shuph his heel." The same verb, asymmetric location. Heel vs. head. Survivable vs. fatal. The wound is mutual but the outcome is not ‚Äî Aphorism [9] (love known by continuity, not symmetry) is encoded in the first wound-word of scripture.

And shuph also means "to cover." The wound is also a covering. The chabburah (stripe = joining, Isa 53:5) anticipated: even here, the wound contains its reversal.

Chavvah ‚Äî Life-Giver Named After Death (v.20)

Chavvah (H2332) = "life-giver." From chavah = to live. Adam names Eve AFTER the fall, AFTER the curse, AFTER "unto dust shalt thou return." In the face of the death-sentence, he calls her Life. The naming is the first act of faith after the fall ‚Äî or the first surplus: from within the pronouncement of death, the name of life.

Labash ‚Äî God's Covering (v.21)

Labash (H3847) = "to wrap around, to clothe." "The LORD God made coats of skins, and labash'd them." The first divine act after pronouncing consequences is to cover the exposed. The covering costs a life (skins = death). Not self-made (unlike the fig leaves of v.7). The initiative is God's. This is katallage (2 Cor 5:18) in miniature and ependuomai (2 Cor 5:4, "clothed upon") anticipated.

The Fall Re-Read Verse by Verse

v.1 ‚Äî "Yea, hath God said?"

The first question after creation attacks the word. The serpent doesn't deny God's existence; he questions God's speech. He attacks the phone (voice, G5456) ‚Äî the medium through which creation was made (Gen 1:3, "God said") and through which recognition comes (John 20:16, "Mary"). If reality is upheld by the word (rhema, Heb 1:3), then questioning the word undermines reality itself. The fall begins as a linguistic event: the word doubted.

v.5 ‚Äî "Ye shall be as gods"

The serpent offers eikon without kenosis. "You shall be like God" ‚Äî but by grasping, not by receiving. The tselem (shadow-image, Gen 1:27) was ALREADY given; the serpent promises elohim through taking what was never denied. This is harpagmos (Phil 2:6, "something to be grasped") ‚Äî the grasping that Christ refused. Adam and Eve grasp; Christ does not. The same offer; opposite responses. The grasping IS the fall. The not-grasping IS the kenosis. Genesis 3 and Philippians 2 are mirror images.

v.6 ‚Äî "She took... and did eat"

Three verbs: saw (ra'ah), took (laqach), ate (akal). Perception ‚Üí appropriation ‚Üí consumption. The seeing becomes acquisitive ‚Äî not the beholding that transforms (2 Cor 3:18, katoptrizomai) but the seeing that takes. The ICT perverted: comparison becomes covetousness rather than illumination.

"Desired to make one wise" ‚Äî haskal (H7919), kin to sakal (prudent/prosperous). The same sakal used of the suffering servant in Isaiah 52:13: "my servant shall deal prudently (sakal)." The wisdom sought by grasping in Eden is the same wisdom achieved by suffering in Isaiah. Same goal. Opposite paths. The fall is the attempt to reach sakal by taking. The servant reaches it by bearing.

v.7 ‚Äî "They knew that they were naked"

The promised "opening" happens ‚Äî but reveals erom (nakedness, from arum/craftiness). Craft produces exposure. And they "knew" (yada, H3045) ‚Äî the same verb God uses in Ps 139:1 (chaqar/yada). But this knowing is self-directed: they know their own shame. The omniscient modality turned inward as self-consciousness rather than God-consciousness. Knowledge that divides rather than connects.

v.8 ‚Äî "They heard the voice of the LORD God"

Qol (H6963) = voice/sound. The same qol that spoke creation into being now walks in the garden. They HEAR before they SEE ‚Äî the voice precedes the encounter, as in John 20:16 (Mary recognized by phone). But now the voice produces fear, not recognition. The phone that should disclose identity triggers hiding. The relational medium is intact; the receivers have broken.

v.9 ‚Äî "Where art thou?"

God's first question to fallen humanity. Not accusatory but locational. The question of the omniscient modality: where in the relational field are you? God doesn't ask for information ‚Äî he asks for self-location. For the human to say where he is. Cf. Ps 139: "thou hast searched me and known me." God knows; the question is whether WE know. The fall is not merely an act but a displacement ‚Äî being somewhere other than where one belongs.

v.10 ‚Äî "I was afraid... and I hid myself"

The first fear. Yare (H3372) ‚Äî the word tracked through the entire study (Synthesis 3): hiding ‚Üí reverence ‚Üí overcome by love. Here at the origin: fear produces hiding. The syn- words (holding-together) are reversed into isolation, fragmentation, concealment. The relational field collapses into self-protection.

v.15 ‚Äî "It shall bruise thy head, thou shalt bruise his heel"

The protoevangelium ‚Äî the first promise of redemption, embedded in a curse. The seed's heel-wound is survivable; the serpent's head-wound is not. Asymmetric. The entire surplus vocabulary anticipated: the wounded one prevails. And shuph = bruise AND cover ‚Äî the wound contains its own reversal.

v.21 ‚Äî "Coats of skins, and clothed them"

The first sacrifice. The first covering that costs a life. The pattern of the entire study compressed into one act:

  • Nakedness exposed ‚Üí God provides covering
  • The covering costs life (animal skins)
  • The covering is not self-made (fig leaves, v.7, were human effort; skins are divine provision)
  • The initiative is entirely God's

The God who will pour out his nephesh (Isa 53:12, arah) and empty himself (Phil 2:7, kenoo) here covers nakedness at the cost of another's life. The kenotic pattern ‚Äî self-emptying to cover another ‚Äî begins in the third chapter of Genesis.

v.24 ‚Äî "To keep the way of the tree of life"

Shamar (H8104) = to keep, guard, preserve. The cherubim don't destroy the way; they GUARD it. The way is preserved ‚Äî concealed but not eliminated. Cf. olam (H5769) = the concealed/everlasting way (Ps 139:24, Ezek 37:26). The way to the tree of life is olam: hidden but everlasting. The garden is sealed, not destroyed. The tree of life persists. Access is not abolished but deferred ‚Äî until the gardener returns (John 20:15, kepouros).

The Deepest Finding

Genesis 3 is not merely the story of what went wrong. Read with the full vocabulary of the study, it is the story of every theme in its embryonic form:

  • The grasping that defines the fall is the mirror-image of the self-emptying that defines kenosis
  • The wound (shuph) already contains the reversal ‚Äî bruise and cover in one word
  • The voice that created still walks in the garden; it is the hearers who have broken
  • The fear that triggers hiding will be transformed through reverence into love
  • The naming of life after death is the first act of faith ‚Äî surplus in the face of sentence
  • The covering that costs is the pattern of all atonement ‚Äî divine initiative, costly, asymmetric
  • The way is hidden, not destroyed ‚Äî guarded for the day the gardener returns

Everything was there from the beginning. The Fall encodes the Restoration.
